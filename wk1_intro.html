What is "case"?

Syntacticians

  • Case refers to licensing.
  • If a noun has case, it is licensed, and so grammatical.
  • If not, then not.
  • Famous instantiation: the case filter.
    • All nouns must receive case.
    • The surface case may or may not match the 'abstract' case.

Morphologists

  • Case refers to the marking on the nouns.
  • The marking reflects what role each item plays in the sentence.
  • Eg. Subjects often appear with a particular case, with objects appearing in another case.
  • Often claimed: this marking aids interpretability of structures:
    • Languages with rich case distinctions often have flexible word orders.

Morphosyntacticians

  • Both are relevant, and the key question is how closely related they are.
  • It is clear that licensing must be accounted for.
  • It is clear that there are regular patterns of case markers.
  • There looks to be some correspondence between having the right case and grammaticality.
  • Let's see how far we can push a combination.

Case and the syntax

Why?

  • Case seems to regulate what can appear where certain items can appear.

    • I bit the crocodile.

    The crocodile bit me.

    *Me bit the crocodile.

    *The crocodile bit I.

Passive Movement

  • Passive sentences involve the notional object becoming the gramamtical subject.

    • I bit the crocodile.

    The crocodile was bitten by me.

  • There is also some change to the verbal morphology: addition of -en + use of 'BE'

Passive Movement

  • With pronouns, we can also see a change in case.

    • The crocodile bit me.

    I was bitten by the crocodile.

  • This change is obligatory!

    • *Me was bitten by the crocodile.

Passive Movement

  • From the preceding we can see two things:
    1. Case seems to have an effect on grammaticality
    2. Case is not dependent on the theta-role of the DP.
  • Hypothesis: Case Filter
    • Case Filter
      *DP if DP does not have case.

Passive Movement

  • Assume that passive verbs lose their ability to assign case.

    • *was bitten the crocodile by me.

    *There was bitten the crocodile by me.

    It was bitten the crocodile by me.

  • If the crocodile moves to Spec,TP to receive nominative, we're good.

    • The crocodile was bitten by me.

ECM vs Raising

Raising

It seems that Nathan is running for office.

Nathan seems to be running for office.

It happens that Martha is busy now.

Martha happens to be busy now.

  • From the above: subjects are not allowed to stay in non-finite Spec,TP.

But, ECM...

I believe that Nathan is the president.

I believe Nathan to be the president.

I believe him to be the president.

*I believe he to be the president.

  • Here, subjects do seem to be allowed in a non-finite Spec,TP.
  • This is only with a subclass of verbs (eg. believe, judge, prove, want, let).

Raising vs ECM

    1. Nominative case is not assigned in Spec,TP.
    2. ECM verbs are special in that they can assign accusative to an embedded Spec,TP.
  • Thus, in Raising constructions:
    • Lower clause = non-finite: subject raises to get nominative.
    • Lower clause = finite: subject stays and gets nominative.

Structural vs inherent case

  • If case is linked to θ-roles, then it is no longer just a structural issue.
  • Whilst θ-roles are by and large structural, the position of a theta role assignment is its underlying position.
  • Structural case refers to the derived position of the element.
  • Is it possible to conflate the two?
